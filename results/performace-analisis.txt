Experimental Results and Performance Analysis

1.0 Introduction to Experimental Framework

The primary objective of these experiments is to rigorously evaluate the performance of a policy-driven expert scheduler managing a UAV's secure communication link. The evaluation was conducted under varying computational loads imposed by different on-board Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) detection models. The analysis encompasses three distinct test families: a baseline scenario with no active DDoS detection, a lightweight mode utilizing an XGBoost classifier, and a heavyweight mode employing a Time-Series Transformer (TST) model. To isolate and clearly assess the performance of the expert policy, all experiments were conducted exclusively with the expert scheduler; Reinforcement Learning (RL) and hybrid scheduling strategies were intentionally disabled for this scope of analysis.

This report presents a detailed examination of the results, beginning with a narrative analysis of key performance indicators. This is followed by comprehensive tabular summaries that allow for direct comparison across cryptographic suites and operational conditions. The analysis covers critical metrics including network performance, cryptographic latencies, system resource utilization, and power consumption, providing a holistic view of the system's behavior under escalating security postures.

2.0 Comprehensive Performance Analysis

This section provides a narrative analysis of the key performance indicators across the three experimental scenarios. The data is interpreted to demonstrate the expert scheduler's behavior and the trade-offs it manages when faced with increasing computational demands from the security subsystems.

2.1 Network Throughput and Link Quality

The viability of a UAV's communication link is primarily indicated by its network throughput, goodput, and packet loss rate. These metrics directly reflect the system's ability to transmit and receive critical data reliably. This analysis compares these key indicators across the Baseline (no DDoS detection), Lightweight (XGBoost), and Heavyweight (TST) scenarios to evaluate the expert scheduler's effectiveness in maintaining link quality under increasing onboard computational stress.

Across all test scenarios, the expert scheduler successfully maintained network throughput at levels consistently close to the target of 8.0 Mb/s, demonstrating its core capability to prioritize data transmission. However, the introduction of the heavyweight TST model introduced a notable trade-off in link quality. While the Baseline and Lightweight scenarios generally exhibited high packet delivery ratios, the Heavyweight scenario saw a marked increase in packet loss. For instance, with the cs-classicmceliece348864-aesgcm-sphincs128fsha2 suite, the packet loss rate increased from 0.040% in the baseline test to a significant 6.447% under the heavy computational load of the TST model. It is important to note that even the Lightweight (XGBoost) model introduced measurable stress, with outliers such as cs-hqc256-aesgcm-mldsa87 and cs-frodokem640aes-chacha20poly1305-mldsa44 experiencing 3.226% and 2.459% loss, respectively. This suggests that the intermittent processing spikes of the XGBoost model, when combined with certain cryptographic workloads, can already begin to saturate system resources. This behavior indicates that as CPU resources become contended, the scheduler begins to shed load by dropping packets—a form of graceful degradation to preserve the primary data-link objective.

In summary, while the scheduler maintains high throughput, the computational overhead of the DDoS detection models measurably impacts link quality, as evidenced by increased packet loss. This degradation in reliability is a direct consequence of resource contention managed by the scheduler, setting the stage for an analysis of the corresponding latency trade-offs.

2.2 Round-Trip Time (RTT) and Latency Profile

For UAV command and control (C2) applications, low and predictable latency, measured by Round-Trip Time (RTT), is critical for mission success and platform stability. This subsection dissects the RTT distributions (average, p50, p95, max) to reveal how the computational overhead of the DDoS detection models impacts communication timeliness.

The results show a clear correlation between computational load and network latency. The Baseline and Lightweight (XGBoost) scenarios maintained stable and low RTTs. In stark contrast, the Heavyweight (TST) scenario introduced significantly higher and more variable RTTs. Using cs-classicmceliece348864-aesgcm-sphincs128fsha2 as a representative example, the average RTT increased from a stable 13.559 ms in the baseline run to 110.382 ms under the heavyweight TST load. An analysis of the tail latency is even more revealing; the 95th percentile (p95) RTT for this suite escalated from 28.647 ms in the baseline to 493.777 ms in the heavyweight test. This dramatic increase in p95 and maximum RTT values is evidence of extreme queuing delays and CPU contention. While the average user experience degrades, the worst-case experience collapses, creating latency spikes that could jeopardize real-time control. This behavior is a direct consequence of the scheduler managing severe resource contention between network processing, cryptographic operations, and the demanding TST model.

The observed increase in network latency is directly compounded by the computational cost of the cryptographic operations themselves, a relationship that is explored in the following section.

2.3 Cryptographic Primitive Performance

The efficiency of the underlying cryptographic operations—specifically key generation, encapsulation, and digital signing—is a primary driver of overall system performance, latency, and power consumption on a resource-constrained platform. This section analyzes the latencies of these primitives to understand the intrinsic computational cost of each Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) suite.

The analysis of the cryptographic breakdown reveals significant performance disparities between the different PQC families. For instance, in the lightweight test scenario, suites based on ML-KEM and ML-DSA, such as cs-mlkem512-chacha20poly1305-mldsa44, exhibit extremely low total primitive latencies, measured at just 0.385 ms. Conversely, suites employing more computationally intensive algorithms, such as cs-classicmceliece8192128-aesgcm-sphincs256fsha2, required 555.850 ms to complete the same set of operations. This difference of three orders of magnitude highlights the vast range of computational costs associated with different PQC algorithms. It is notable that under the extreme load of the heavyweight scenario, the reported latencies for very fast primitives can become anomalously low, likely due to measurement artifacts or scheduler-induced timing distortions under near-100% CPU saturation. These intrinsic performance characteristics are nonetheless critical inputs for the scheduler, as they directly influence its decisions when balancing security requirements against real-time operational constraints.

The high computational cost of certain primitives is a leading contributor to overall system resource utilization, which is examined next.

2.4 System Resource Utilization

On a resource-constrained UAV platform, meticulous monitoring of CPU and memory utilization is essential to ensure system stability. The Resident Set Size (RSS) provides a measure of the memory footprint, while maximum CPU utilization indicates peak computational load. This analysis assesses the impact of each DDoS detection model on system load, providing clear insight into the overhead imposed by each defensive posture.

The experimental data reveals a clear and consistent trend of increasing resource consumption as the security posture escalates. In the baseline scenario, the memory footprint (RSS) was generally contained within 270-280 MiB. Activating the lightweight XGBoost model increased this to a range of 600-615 MiB. The introduction of the heavyweight TST model further increased the memory footprint to a range of 742-780 MiB across the test suites. For the cs-classicmceliece348864-aesgcm-sphincs128fsha2 suite, RSS memory grew from 268.4 MiB (no-ddos) to 605.2 MiB (xgboost) and finally to 749.0 MiB (transformer). A similar trend was observed in CPU utilization. While the baseline and lightweight modes saw peak CPU usage in the 70-85% range, the heavyweight TST scenario consistently pushed the CPU toward saturation, with maximum utilization frequently exceeding 90%.

As expected, increased system load invariably leads to higher power consumption, a critical constraint for battery-powered UAVs that directly impacts mission endurance.

2.5 Power and Energy Consumption

For any unmanned aerial vehicle, power consumption is a paramount concern, as it directly dictates flight time and operational range. This subsection analyzes the average power draw (in Watts) and total energy consumed (in Joules) to quantify the energy cost of running different PQC suites and DDoS detection models.

The results demonstrate a clear, incremental increase in power consumption corresponding to the complexity of the active DDoS detection model. Using cs-classicmceliece348864-aesgcm-sphincs128fsha2 as an example, the average power draw rose from 4.234 W in the baseline scenario to 4.354 W with the lightweight XGBoost model, and further to 4.585 W with the heavyweight TST model. This trend holds across all tested PQC suites, confirming that the heightened computational and memory load required by the more advanced security models translates directly into increased energy expenditure.

In conclusion, the experimental results reveal a clear and quantifiable set of trade-offs between the deployed security posture and key operational performance metrics. The expert scheduler is designed to navigate these trade-offs, demonstrating an ability to maintain primary functionality through graceful degradation under severe computational stress.

4.0 Analysis of Performance Trends

This section provides a narrative interpretation of key performance trends identified from the aggregated experimental data.

4.1 Trend 1: Throughput and Goodput vs. DDoS Defense Mode

DDoS Policy	Average Throughput (Mb/s)	Average Packet Loss (%)
Baseline	7.476	0.655
Lightweight	7.886	0.697
Heavyweight	7.632	3.637

The data shows that average throughput remains remarkably stable across all three defense modes. This highlights the scheduler's success in prioritizing data transmission. However, this stability comes at the cost of link quality when the heavyweight TST model is active. The average packet loss rate increases more than five-fold from the Lightweight to the Heavyweight scenario. This trend indicates that as the system's CPU becomes saturated by the TST model, the scheduler implements a graceful degradation strategy, sacrificing packet reliability to maintain a consistent data rate and prevent a total collapse of the communication link.

The data reveals a consistent trend of increasing energy consumption as the DDoS policy escalates from Baseline to Lightweight and finally to Heavyweight, a pattern that holds true for all PQC families. While individual cryptographic suites have vastly different computational costs (as shown in Section 2.3), the average power consumption across the ML-KEM (4.632 W), Classic-McEliece (4.634 W), and HQC (4.636 W) families is functionally identical. This indicates that the constant, high-utilization workload of the TST model dominates the system's power signature. The DDoS model's overhead becomes the primary driver of power consumption, raising the entire system's power floor and masking the more subtle performance differences between the cryptographic algorithms.

The data reveals a clear, stepwise increase in both CPU and memory utilization as the DDoS policy is escalated. Moving from the Baseline to the Lightweight (XGBoost) policy results in a marginal increase in peak CPU load but more than doubles the memory footprint. The transition to the Heavyweight (TST) policy introduces a substantial burden, pushing the average peak CPU utilization above 90% and further increasing memory consumption by over 150 MiB. This analysis confirms that the heavyweight TST model imposes a significant and consistent resource overhead, which is the primary driver for the performance trade-offs observed in other key metrics like latency and packet loss.

4.4 Trend 4: RTT Latency vs. Cryptographic Intensity

This trend analysis demonstrates a compounding effect between intrinsic cryptographic workload and overall system load on network latency. The data shows that suites with higher intrinsic cryptographic processing times experience a more dramatic increase in network RTT when the system is under heavy load from the TST model. A high-cost primitive occupies the CPU for a longer, continuous duration. When this operation coincides with the TST model's processing window, it creates a prolonged "CPU unavailable" state, causing network packet queues to build up significantly and thus leading to dramatic RTT spikes. For instance, cs-mlkem768-aesgcm-mldsa65, with a low primitive latency of 1.993 ms, sees its RTT increase by a factor of 2.8x. In contrast, cs-hqc192-aesgcm-mldsa65, with a much higher primitive latency of 145.115 ms, experiences a 3.5x increase in RTT, clearly illustrating this compounding effect.

Focusing exclusively on link reliability, the packet loss trend underscores the operational cost of the heavyweight security posture. The nominal packet loss observed in the Baseline (~0.66%) and Lightweight (~0.70%) scenarios indicates a highly reliable link where the system can comfortably handle both network traffic and moderate computational tasks. The sharp increase to an average loss of 3.64% in the Heavyweight scenario signifies that the system has reached a saturation point. At this stage, the combined demands of the TST model, cryptographic operations, and network packet processing exceed available CPU capacity, forcing the scheduler to shed load by dropping packets to prevent a complete system stall.

5.0 Synthesis

The experimental analysis confirms that the policy-driven expert scheduler effectively manages the trade-offs inherent in deploying advanced security capabilities on a resource-constrained UAV platform. The system demonstrates graceful degradation under the severe computational stress imposed by the heavyweight Time-Series Transformer DDoS detection model. It successfully maintains high data throughput by strategically trading off increased network latency and a higher packet loss rate. The results clearly quantify the direct relationships between security posture, communication performance, system resource utilization, and energy consumption. Activating more sophisticated defense models invariably leads to higher CPU and memory usage, which in turn increases power draw and degrades real-time network characteristics. It is important to note that these results are confined to the performance of the expert scheduler. A comparative evaluation of Reinforcement Learning (RL) or hybrid scheduling strategies, which may offer different adaptive behaviors, falls outside the scope of this analysis.
